Value Date and Position Roll-Over

Commonly accepted in the Forex market and under margin trading conditions are settlements (Value Date) two days after the transaction is made. A transaction with a settlement date two days after the transaction is called SPOT. In the currency market, there are TOD (today - settlements today), TOM (tomorrow - settlements tomorrow), and other transactions, but they are not used under margin trading conditions. Thus, the settlement date for the transaction is firmly fixed at the time it is made.

It often happens that after opening a transaction today, you cannot or do not want to close it with a profit, which creates the need to extend the settlements for the open position to a later date. This operation is called a position roll-over, and intermediaries (banks) charge a certain commission for its execution - a SWAP . In real conditions of the interbank Forex market, the amount of the roll-over commission depends on the difference in current rates for holding each of the currencies involved in the transaction. At the same time, the position roll-over can even bring profit.

When working under margin trading conditions, the trader deposits collateral with the intermediary, which serves as security for the settlements of transactions. At the same time, the bank providing Forex trading services constantly calculates the current result of the transactions opened by the client to avoid a situation where the client's losses exceed the deposited amount. Thus, if the client's losses reach a certain level, the bank either automatically and forcibly closes the open positions or issues a requirement for additional funds to be deposited.

This requirement to deposit funds is called a margin-call. Different banks-market makers have different account balance levels at which a margin-call or forced closure occurs, ranging from 10 to 40 percent of the total deposit amount. A large deposit with a balanced strategy and transaction volumes allows better holding temporarily unprofitable positions and provides a margin of safety in case of temporary unfavorable rate fluctuations.
