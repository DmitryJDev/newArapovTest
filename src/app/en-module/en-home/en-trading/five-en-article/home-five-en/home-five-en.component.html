Derivatives and Their Types

Derivative financial instruments traded on exchanges.

Derivative Financial Instrument (Derivative)

A financial instrument whose value is based on obligations related to other investment assets or commodities. In simple terms, a derivative is a security based on another security. There are several main types of derivative securities: futures, forward contracts, swaps, options, swaptions, and contracts for differences.

Derivatives are categorically divided according to the assets underlying them:

Financial Derivative Securities

Contracts based on interest rates for short-term and long-term bonds in the US, UK, and other countries.

Currency Derivative Securities

Contracts on the exchange rate of euro/dollar, dollar/yen, and other global currencies.

Index Derivative Securities

Contracts on stock indices such as S&P 500, Nasdaq 100, FTSE 100.

Commodity Derivative Securities

Contracts for energy resources, such as oil, for precious metals—gold, platinum, palladium, silver—for base metals—aluminum, nickel—and for agricultural products—wheat, soybeans, meat, coffee, cocoa, and even concentrated orange juice.

Only a small percentage of transactions (up to 3%) involve actual delivery of the underlying assets of derivative securities. Transactions are primarily settled through mutual cash settlements via the clearing process.

Derivative securities serve two main purposes.

Firstly, they are used for financial risk hedging. For example, an agricultural producer can protect against future price declines of their produce when planning for harvest. Or an automotive company needing a known quantity of base metal for future production can hedge against its price increase over a specific period.

Secondly, derivative securities are among the most profitable instruments for speculative trading operations. For instance, to buy a futures contract for €100,000 against the US dollar with delivery in three months, a deposit of approximately $2,000 is required. This allows leveraging a significant amount of funds essentially for free, known as "trading on margin." Unfortunately, speculative trading with derivative securities is among the riskiest compared to other financial instruments.
