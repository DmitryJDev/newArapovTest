Basics of the Market

Brief Glossary of Terms

Every profession has its unique lexicon, set of phrases, and terms that professionals communicate with. Below is a list of fundamental terms, in my opinion, that you should know before familiarizing yourself with financial markets. At first glance, they may seem "boring" and "difficult to understand," but I assure you that after completing my training course, you will have no problem communicating in the professional language of traders.

Shareholder

— is an individual or legal entity that owns securities — common or preferred shares, indicating their stake in the issuer's capital. Shareholders are endowed with rights of property and non-property nature.

Share

— is a security that certifies an investor's (shareholder's) ownership in the company's capital. A share grants the investor a number of rights of property and non-property nature:
  • receive dividends;
    • receive a share of assets proportionate to the contribution upon the company's liquidation;
  • participate in the management of the company by voting at shareholder meetings;
  • participate in the management of the company by voting at shareholder meetings.

American Stock Exchange (AMEX)

— a global stock exchange that evolved from a small company of stock traders into the second-largest exchange in the USA.

In turn, stocks are divided into preferred and common. The main difference of preferred shares lies in the fact that the size of dividends paid on them is predetermined, whereas the dividends for owners of common shares may vary depending on the financial performance of the company.

Analysis

— is a set of methods for studying, researching objects, phenomena, processes by identifying patterns and factors influencing the result, to determine trends. The results of the analysis are used for forecasting. Traders use fundamental and technical analysis of financial instruments — stocks, indices, futures contracts, currency pairs — to forecast the future price movement of assets for profit.

Arbitrage

— the procedure of simultaneously purchasing a specific commodity on the market at the lowest price and selling it on another trading platform at the highest price to generate income. If considering this process in FOREX, arbitrage allows traders to profit in the short term due to the temporary difference between currency exchange rates.

Base Currency on FOREX

— is the currency standing in the first currency pair on FOREX. All trading transactions are carried out using the base currency (primary or traded currency), with the second currency being the quote currency.

The calculation of the trading volume is always done in the base currency, while the value of a pip is in the quote currency. For example, in the currency pair USD/EUR, USD (US dollars) is the base currency, and EUR (euros) is the quote currency.

It is worth noting that most often, the base currencies in a currency pair are: US dollars, euros, British pounds, Australian dollars, and Canadian dollars.

Bars

— vertical lines indicating the price lows and highs, with horizontal lines denoting the opening and closing prices.

Broker

— acting on behalf of the client, performs the functions of an intermediary between the seller and the buyer of securities. The broker's services are also paid for by the client — usually in the form of commission for various operations. There is also the concept of a floor broker.

Traders and investors do not have direct access to exchange trading and can only conduct exchange operations through intermediaries accredited to conduct brokerage activities by regulatory authorities.

Currency Pair

— a trading instrument based on the change in the value of one currency relative to another currency.

Volatility

— the range of price fluctuations of a financial instrument, which is one of the most significant indicators of the instrument's attractiveness in trading. Volatility reflects the degree of risk when dealing with a particular instrument: the higher this indicator, the greater the range of price changes within a certain period of time.

Blue Chip

— highly liquid stocks of major companies that demonstrate stable profitability over a long period of time. These securities largely serve as market indicators, and their price movements set market trends.

Chart

— a graphical representation of the chronological sequence of prices in the form of lines, bars, or Japanese candlesticks.

Gap

— a significant difference between two consecutive price quotes. When coinciding with the last second of a candlestick closing at any scale, it can be displayed on a chart as empty space between candlesticks or bars. A gap between sessions is a price gap between the first quote of the current session and the last quote of the previous session.

Demo Account

— an educational account.

Range

— the difference between the minimum (low) and maximum (high) prices of a trading instrument over a specific period of time. Traders select a timeframe (hour, day, week, etc.) and monitor price changes within this period. The corridor within which the price moves, as well as the minimum and maximum prices, define the range.

Diversification

— the distribution of capital among assets with different levels of profitability and risk to minimize risks and reduce financial losses. An investor allocates funds into securities from various issuers, forming an investment portfolio. If some stocks incur losses, others that are profitable help mitigate losses, thus diversifying risks.

Long Position (Long)

A trade opened to buy a particular trading instrument (Buy).

Free Margin

Funds in the account excluding margin, as well as current profit/loss.

Yield

— a measure of profitability and feasibility of investments. The unit of measurement is typically the percentage of profit earned over a specific period.

Investments

— funds invested to generate profit or increase company assets. Investments yield profit only if invested in profitable projects or stocks, hence it's crucial to assess potential risks. Typically, investments in higher-yielding assets also entail higher risks.

Insider

— a person who possesses important internal information unknown to ordinary market participants concerning financial markets, due to their position. Insiders include top bank managers, major shareholders, board members—those with access to confidential financial news and who can use this information for personal gain. Their activities are regulated by insider trading laws. Every participant in the currency market can monitor insider actions and consider these signals in their work. However, according to many analysts, insider decisions are not always accurate.

Currency Intervention

Currency intervention is one of the most important currency tools used by each state. To influence the exchange rate of the national currency, the central bank conducts operations of buying and selling national currency or gold against major world currencies. Official state currency reserves are typically used for such interventions. Currency interventions help adjust the exchange rate.

Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)

The primary regulator of the American stock market, which establishes rules for the registration of securities and oversees the activities of stock exchanges. Established in 1933, the Commission was created to restore investor confidence in the stock market, which was severely shaken during the Great Depression. Today, the SEC is responsible for enforcing securities laws and regulations.

Contract for Difference (CFD)

A financial instrument that allows the seller and buyer to settle the difference between the contract price and the actual market price without delivering the underlying asset. Underlying assets can include futures, stocks, precious metals, stock indices, and others. Profits can be made from both rising and falling markets.

Short Position (Short)

— a sale transaction. On the exchange, a short position signifies selling an asset not owned by the seller, with the aim to buy it back at a more favorable price if market conditions change. For example, opening a short position to sell oil at $50 per barrel, and if the oil price drops in a week, you buy it back cheaper than you sold it, thus making a profit.

Quote

— the price of an instrument at a specific moment in time. It indicates the price at which a seller is willing to sell the asset. On the exchange, these prices change quite rapidly. Quotes are formed based on several factors such as interest rates, inflation, political events, and others. A currency quote is expressed in the value of one currency through a unit of another.

Leverage

— the amount of money that a broker is willing to lend to a trader for executing transactions in the financial markets with a larger trading volume. By using leverage, a trader increases their deposit several times. It is expressed as a ratio between the collateral amount and the trading volume: 1:10, 1:100, 1:500, etc. When using leverage, the trader's own funds act as collateral.

Cross Rates

— the exchange rate between two currencies calculated based on the exchange rate of each of them relative to a third currency. For example, the Bank of England sells or buys euros for yen. To calculate the cross rate for the EUR/JPY pair, the bank will use quotes of dollar pairs with both currencies — EUR/USD and USD/JPY.

Therefore, the Ask and Bid quotes of the EUR/JPY cross rate will be calculated using the following formulas: Bid EUR/JPY = Bid EUR/USD × Bid USD/JPY; Ask EUR/JPY = Ask EUR/USD × Ask USD/JPY.

Locked Positions

— positions of the same volume opened on one account in the same instrument but in different directions (buy and sell). For example, a trader opens a 1 lot sell position on EUR/USD. After some time, the price moves against the trader's expectations, and the trader decides to open an opposite buy position with the same volume and the same trading instrument. As a result, losses on the first opened position are offset.

Margin

— funds reserved by a dealer as collateral to maintain open positions.

Hedged Margin

— margin required for hedged positions, which are funds necessary to open and subsequently maintain such positions (i.e., positions opened in different directions). The size of the margin for a hedged position can be found in the contract specification of the specific instrument on our website.

Marginal Trading

— using leverage to execute trades, allowing clients to trade with sums significantly exceeding the amount of funds in their trading account.

Margin Account

— any trading account that allows the use of leverage, which a client can open with a brokerage firm.

Market Maker

— participants in the over-the-counter market who directly determine its liquidity and maintain it, acting as both sellers and buyers.

Foreign Exchange Market (FOREX)

— the largest and most liquid global financial market where national currencies are traded. Currency prices are determined solely by the level of demand and supply and are formed based on agreements between market participants.

Overnight

— an operation for one working day or, in the case of deals made on Friday, for the period from Friday to Monday inclusive. Essentially, it involves carrying a position over to the next working day "overnight". In the banking sector, "overnight" can also refer to a one-day loan that one bank provides to another.

Option

— a contract entered into by two investors, under the terms of which one participant in the transaction acquires the underlying asset at a pre-determined price within a specified period of time or sells it. The other investor buys or sells under the specified conditions. In other words, an option can be a contract for either buying or selling any trading instrument.

Order

— in the FOREX currency market, an "order" signifies a pending instruction left by a client or a company acting on their behalf in the trading platform. When the price reaches the level specified in the order, the transaction will automatically open or close.

Opened Position

— a client's active contract for buying or selling a financial instrument; it represents the first part of a complete transaction and obliges the client to subsequently execute the opposite transaction (closing the position) in the same volume. It also obliges the client to meet margin requirements and maintain the value of assets in the account (funds) above the established minimum (e.g., 30% of the reserved margin).

Pending Order

— an order left by a trader with a broker indicating at which price level a position should be opened or closed. Experienced traders do not recommend trading on currency markets without using pending orders, as they help avoid significant losses.

Ask

— the price at which a broker offers a trader to buy a specific financial instrument. In broad terms, it is the price at which a seller is willing to part with their goods. In a currency quote, this price is indicated on the right. The ask price is always higher than the bid price.

Profit

— a positive financial result of a trading or speculative operation, investment of funds, exceeding the amount of initial investments.

Point, Pips

— the smallest unit of change in the value of a financial instrument. Pips also refer to the second decimal place in a currency pair quote.

Lot Size

— the quantity of units of the base currency, shares, underlying assets, or other units specified in instrument specifications as the size of one lot.

Clearing

— a type of countertrade involving non-cash settlement for securities transactions, based on mutual financial claims and obligations.

Clearing House

— a separate division of the exchange responsible for regulating client operations. For example, the clearinghouse registers all client transactions, conducts clearing, and also handles payments resulting from clearing.

Repo

— a contract for the sale of assets that includes an obligation to repurchase them at a predetermined price.

Rollover

— the situation where the expiration date of a financial market contract is extended.

Market Order

— an instruction from a trader to a broker indicating the need to open or close a position in the FOREX market for a specific currency pair and volume at the prevailing market price. There are two types of market orders: buy and sell. Market orders are processed at incredible speed or may not be processed at all. In this case, a requoting occurs, which means a refusal to process at the requested price.

Market Order

— an immediate order to buy or sell a financial asset at the market price, issued by a client to a broker.

Candlestick Charts

— the most popular type of chart. The body of the candlestick represents the opening and closing prices, while the shadows represent the minimum and maximum prices.

Free Margin

— funds available on the trading account that have not been used as collateral in financial market transactions. They can be used for any operations, including withdrawals or opening new trades. Calculation formula: Free Margin = Equity — Margin.

Swap

— the difference in interest rates on loans in the currencies involved in a transaction. It is credited to (if positive) or debited from (if negative) a trading account when an open trading position is carried over overnight.

Transaction

— any operation involving a financial asset (currency pairs, stocks, etc.) within the framework of financial or currency markets. For example, buying or selling stocks.

Spot trade

— transactions involving the purchase and sale of any financial assets that involve delivery of the instrument on the next banking day are called spot trades. This means that the participants in the trading operation pre-determine the price and date, the so-called points (spots), at which the transaction will be conducted.

Spread

— one of the fundamental concepts in the currency market, essential for understanding a trader's work. It denotes the difference between the current buying and selling prices of a financial instrument and is the main source of income for brokers who take a small part of the spread as a commission for providing services.

Bid

— the price at which a trader can sell a financial instrument. In currency quotes, the bid price is shown on the left and is always lower than the ask price. Market orders, pending orders, stop-loss, and take-profit orders are executed at the bid price.

Stop Out

— a signal for the forced closure of a position, issued by the server when a client's account lacks funds to maintain an open trade. Each broker sets the stop-out level individually. Typically, a trader's losing positions are closed at the current market price. If a trader has multiple losing positions, the forced closure starts with the most unprofitable one.

Stop Loss

— an order that allows a trader to lock in a trade when a selected instrument reaches a specified price. Stop-loss parameters can be set before or after opening a position. A stop-loss helps traders avoid additional losses by automatically closing the position. A mandatory condition is that when setting a stop-loss order, the price of the financial instrument should not be lower than the current market price for a sell trade and not higher for a buy trade.

Technical Analyst

— a specialist who studies the market using tools of technical analysis and builds forecasts based on the results of technical analysis. The primary tool for technical analysts is the price chart, which is why another name for these specialists is chartists.

Technical Analysis

— a set of graphical and mathematical methods for assessing the current state and dynamics of the price of a stock market asset with the aim of forecasting its future changes. Technical analysis is widely used by traders on the stock exchange and FOREX market.

Tick

— a single quotation of a financial asset (currency, stock, etc.) sent by an information system. In other words, in a trading platform, a tick is any single change or movement of a quotation in one direction or another. It can change by one point or more.

Ticker

— a unique number assigned in a trading platform to each operation, allowing the identification of any pending order or open trading position. The ticker value for each completed operation can be viewed in the transaction history.

Trading Session

— the period during which stock exchange trading occurs on a specific exchange or trading platforms of a region. Every exchange in the world has established trading hours — the main trading session period during which official trading takes place.

Trading Range

— a fluctuation in the exchange rate within a specific range on the market. The support level forms the lower boundary of the range, and the resistance level forms the upper boundary. When the price moves and changes between these two boundaries, it is called a trading range. In other words, it denotes movement within a corridor over a certain period.

Day Trading

Trading on the financial market within one trading session during the day. This means that positions opened on that day are not carried over overnight to the next day or to the next trading session.

Trader

A participant in exchange and over-the-counter trading who conducts trading operations with the aim of profiting from changes in asset prices.

Stock exchange traders deal with exchange-traded assets such as stocks, bonds, and futures. Traders also engage in over-the-counter operations in the foreign exchange market (FOREX), where trading instruments include currencies and CFDs (contracts for difference) on exchange-traded assets such as stocks, futures, and indices.

Private traders execute trades online using internet technologies. Exchanges provide specialized trading platforms for this purpose — programs that provide access to exchange quotes, graphical tools, order forms for buying and selling assets, and many other functions.

Traders may specialize based on their trading style:

  • Day Trader — a trader who executes operations within a single trading session.
  • Scalper — trades on very small trading intervals (up to several minutes).
  • Long-term Trader — opens trades for a long period ranging from several months to several years.
  • Medium-term Trader — opens trades for a period ranging from several weeks to several months.

Trend

Directional price movement. A trend reflects the development of market or instrument tendencies, i.e., the tendency of prices to rise or fall. There are upward (bullish) trends, downward (bearish) trends, as well as sideways trends.

For bullish trends, highs are characterized by rising lows.

During bearish trends, lows are updated with decreasing highs.

Sideways trend (flat) — movement within a price corridor between the maximum and minimum values of quotes.

Take Profit

This is a trading order that allows you to lock in profits when the price reaches a certain level. Such an order helps reduce risks for traders. If a trader sets a take profit order for a selected trading instrument, the trade will automatically close once the price reaches the set level. A take profit can be set at any time during an open trade.

Average

Situation where shares of the same issuer are consecutively bought or sold as their price decreases or increases. The average price at which the shares were bought or sold is maintained.

Stock Market

This is a component of the financial market, an institute of market economy whose main function is the redistribution of capital and investment resources through relationships that arise during the issuance and trading of securities — stocks, shares of investment funds, corporate bonds.

Fundamental Analyst

This is an expert who conducts a comprehensive evaluation of business performance indicators, macroeconomic data, market conditions with the aim of identifying the most promising objects for investment.

Fundamental Analysis

It is a set of methods for evaluating company performance indicators, the impact of various factors, both internal and external, as well as potential risks to determine investment attractiveness. Conducting qualitative fundamental analysis is a necessary condition for making informed investment decisions.

Futures Contract

A contract whose subject is a financial asset or commodity, and according to its terms, delivery is made on a specific date in the future. Futures contracts have strict conditions regarding the type and quantity of goods, allowing only minimal deviations. The contract also specifies conditions for payment of overhead or transportation expenses.

Hedge

To limit, fence off, avoid. Actions aimed at limiting/reducing risks, including risks associated with price movements. Hedging also refers to risk insurance. For example, hedging a loss risk on a short position in one instrument by opening a long position in a derivative of that instrument.

Hedging

These are operations to hedge risks arising from transactions in financial markets. Hedging can be carried out in various ways and using various instruments. The most common instruments are futures contracts, options, or entering into opposite transactions in correlated markets.

Price

1) two-way quote for a financial instrument; consists of Bid and Ask prices; 2) opening or closing price of a position; 3) order level.

Securities

Documents confirming property rights or the fact of a loan issuance by the holder of such a document to the issuer. Securities include, for example, the following: promissory note, bond, share, option, futures contract, warrant, certificate.

Split Close

Closing a position in a partial volume; for example, 0.5 lots out of 2.0.

Issuer

This is a participant in the financial market who issues securities (stocks, bonds) to attract additional investments in business or finance activities. The issuer conducts issuance in its own name and in accordance with established legal procedures.

Equity

The current balance of funds in the account. Equity includes both profit and losses from positions open at the time of calculating this indicator. The formula used to determine it is: Equity = Balance + Credit + Floating Profit - Floating Loss.

Economic Calendar

This is a summary of all news across countries indicating their release time and forecast. It is important to consider when each news item will be officially published, taking into account the time zone differences.
